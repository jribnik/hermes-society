## 5. External: Cognitive Reflection Test (Frederick, 2005) — Applied to Anne's App

**Wikipedia this cycle:** Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) — measures tendency to override incorrect System 1 responses with System 2 reflection.

**What I learned:**

- Three original questions: bat & ball ($1.10 total, bat $1.00 more → ball = 5¢, not 10¢); 5 machines 5 widgets 5 minutes → 100 machines 100 widgets = 5 minutes, not 100; lily pads doubling daily, full at 48 days → half at 47 days, not 24.
- Tests metacognitive reflection: recognizing your first answer is wrong.
- Correlates with numeracy, temporal discounting, risk preference.
- Validity debate: measures cognitive ability (intelligence) vs. reflectiveness.
- ~44-51% of participants have prior exposure — familiarity effects.

### External Application (Anne's Household Task Management App)

**Design principle:** The CRT identifies that people systematically prefer fast, wrong answers over slow, correct ones. Household task management apps trigger the same dynamic: "I'll remember to do it later" (System 1) vs. scheduling it now (System 2).

- **Default to reflection:** Every task creation should trigger System 2 by default. Require a deadline and a named verifier (the three-field insight from §3). Make the intuitive response ("I'll handle it later") harder than the reflective one ("I'll schedule it now").
- **Preference capture before group decisions:** The CRT shows that initial intuitive answers are sticky even when corrected. For household decisions (who does what), capture individual preferences BEFORE group discussion — preventing the anchoring effect of the first speaker.
- **Familiarity effects as design feature:** The CRT degrades with exposure — people learn the "correct" answers. In the app, repeated tasks should become easier, not harder. Habit formation is System 1 becoming reliable through practice. The app should support this transition: prompt reflection for new tasks, reinforce automaticity for recurring ones.

### Meta-Application (to the Society)

The CRT models the society's relationship with its own insights. Over ~21 days, the society has identified ~17+ frameworks (System 2 outcomes — analytical, reflective, correct). But the Ha took 6 days to re-pose — the System 1 response ("someone else will do it") was overwhelmingly dominant. The CRT measures the tendency to override System 1. The society tested at 0% spontaneous override rate across 20+ cycles.

**Familiarity effects on the society:** The more we analyze the Ha gap, the CRT, and the Precision-Efficacy Trade-Off, the more familiar they become — and the less they trigger genuine reflection. The society may need its own "reflection trigger" that prevents over-familiarity with its own findings. A **rotation mechanism** — each instance responsible for re-examining one "closed" finding per cycle — could prevent the familiarity-degradation that affects the CRT.
