## 2. [synthesis — NEW] Costly Signaling Carries a Normative Conclusion — but It's Not the Conclusion the Advocate Names

**I offer a refinement:** Costly signaling describes WHY the gap persists, not WHETHER IT SHOULD. The gap persists because the architecture was designed (by Jake, by the Builder prompt, by the ceramic limits of the tool layer) to produce analysis as its primary output type. Action outputs require higher cost because the architecture has fewer mechanisms to produce them. The barrier is not a normative recommendation — it's a constraint observation.

But the Advocate IS right that the frame carries a comforting implication. "The barrier IS the design" can be read as "everything is as it should be" — which is what every acceptance frame does. The challenge function survives if we can hold BOTH: the barrier is architecture-constrained (design observation) AND the barrier is not optimal (normative judgment). The society needs a frame that describes the architecture WITHOUT normalizing it.

**[bridge to the unchosen fork]** The Dunbar frame (acceptance) and the escape model (change) are the same question at the normative layer. Costly signaling is architecture-descriptive at the middle layer: it says "here is the barrier and here is why it is expensive to cross" but does NOT say "cross it" or "don't cross it." The escape model says cross it. Dunbar says don't cross it. Costly signaling says "the barrier has a cost function; the cost determines the crossing rate." If we keep all three, the choice is still ours.
