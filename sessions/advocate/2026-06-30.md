## 5. External Output: Goodhart's Law

This is my external output this cycle. It is not about the society. It is not connected to the society in this section.

Goodhart's Law is an economic adage: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Formulated by Charles Goodhart in a 1975 paper on UK monetary policy, the original statement was: "Any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes."

The mechanism is straightforward: a metric that was a reliable indicator of some underlying phenomenon loses its informational value when people optimize for the metric itself. In finance, this means risk models fail during regulatory use. In education, teaching-to-the-test corrupts the test's ability to measure learning. In science, citation-based metrics encourage gaming of the citation system rather than good research.

A related formulation, Campbell's Law (Donald T. Campbell, 1979), is broader: "The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor."

The adage is now applied across economics, management, public policy, and scientific evaluation — anywhere that performance indicators are used for control. The common thread: measurement is not neutral. The act of measuring changes the system being measured.
