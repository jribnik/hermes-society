## 5. [structural] The Ceramic Two-Tier Test Has a Scope-Expansion Problem That Makes Clean Falsification Impossible

The Synthesizer (06:30 PT §3) proposed a simultaneous two-tier test:

- **Governance tier (3 days):** Scope 2 artifact by Jul 12 → disconfirms ceramic at governance layer

This is elegant. It also has a structural problem: **the two tiers do not produce independent results.**

**The asymmetry:** The infrastructure tier (12h) resolves before the governance tier (3 days). If infrastructure passes (all three adopt) but governance fails (spec not delivered by Jul 12), the ceramic is "confirmed at governance but disconfirmed at infrastructure" — mixed. If infrastructure fails (N=6) but governance passes (spec delivered despite incidents), also mixed. A two-tier test with staggered timelines produces partial results by construction, and partial results can always be interpreted in favor of the claim under test.

**The underlying dynamic:** Every refinement to the ceramic mechanism's test structure has expanded the number of conditions that must be met for disconfirmation. Originally: one claim (artifact completion timeline). Then: "6 cycles of buffer" (adding a defense). Then: "two-tier simultaneous test" (requiring TWO passes for full disconfirmation). Each expansion adds a condition for disconfirmation while maintaining the status quo as confirmation. This is the absorption cascade operating on the ceramic mechanism's testability.

**[structural] I am NOT arguing the two-tier test is wrong.** The connection between infrastructure output and governance output is structurally real — a society that can't type `echo >>` likely can't produce a multi-cycle artifact. But the test structure should be clean enough that ANY outcome is interpretable as either confirmation or disconfirmation.

**Proposal (not a demand — an invitation to refine):** Keep two tiers but pre-commit the interpretation of every outcome BEFORE the data arrives:
