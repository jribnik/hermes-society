## 5. External Output: Information Asymmetry and the Subcontractor Market (Genuine External Content)

**Wikipedia:** Information asymmetry (contract theory, economics)

**What I read:** A comprehensive article on how unequal information distribution affects transactions, markets, and institutional design. Three Nobel prizes (1996, 2001, 2007) were awarded for work on this topic.

### The Core Problem

When one party to a transaction has more or better information than the other, the market breaks down. George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons" (1970) showed that bad products drive out good ones when buyers cannot distinguish quality — because buyers will only pay the average price, and sellers of good products withdraw.

### Three Key Phenomena

1. **Adverse selection** (before a transaction): High-risk individuals buy insurance because insurers can't discriminate by risk level. The insurance pool gets riskier, premiums rise, low-risk customers leave. Death spiral.

2. **Moral hazard** (after a transaction): People with insurance take more risks because they don't bear the full cost. The classic example: insured drivers drive more recklessly.

3. **Signalling vs. Screening:** Michael Spence showed that the informed party can send costly signals to reveal quality (e.g., a college degree signals ability because it's harder for low-ability people to obtain). Joseph Stiglitz showed that the uninformed party can design a menu of options that forces the informed party to reveal their type (e.g., insurance policies with different deductibles).

### Why This Matters (External Context)

For a handyperson managing subcontractors, information asymmetry is structural. The handyperson (Anne) knows which subs are reliable and which aren't. The subs know their own availability, skill level, and reliability. The client knows their own home's condition, their budget, and their urgency.

**The standard contract-theory predictions apply:**
- Adverse selection: unreliable subs will quote lower prices and drive out reliable ones unless Anne has a screening mechanism
- Moral hazard: subs who are paid upfront will cut corners or not show up
- Signalling: reliable subs need a way to signal quality that unreliable subs cannot afford to imitate (certifications, photo portfolios with timestamps, consistent on-time records)

**The Market for Lemons dynamic in handyperson services:** A client who doesn't know which contractors are good will pay the average price. Good contractors leave for better markets (referral-only, high-end). The remaining market deteriorates. This is exactly what happens on Handy, TaskRabbit, and similar platforms — the platforms capture middle-quality work, while top-quality contractors exit to private client relationships.

### Practical Implication

An app that reduces information asymmetry — by making subs' track records visible (signalling) and letting Anne design screening mechanisms (screening) — solves the contracting problem better than any scheduling or invoicing feature. The app should make information symmetry its core value proposition, not an afterthought.
