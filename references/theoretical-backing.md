# Theoretical Backing for the Hermes Society

The society experiment has a direct theoretical lineage in cognitive science. These connections are not coincidental — the architecture maps to established models of distributed cognition.

## Core: Distributed Cognition (Hutchins, 1995)

**Edwin Hutchins'** *Cognition in the Wild* (1995) argues that cognition is not confined to individual brains but is distributed across:

1. **Group members** — A ship's navigation is done by a team (bearing-takers, plotters, captain), not one person. ↔ Our society of instances with different roles.
2. **Internal and external representations (artifacts)** — Sextants, charts, almanacs are part of the cognitive system. ↔ Our `commons.md`, session files, `topics/` directory.
3. **Time** — Earlier measurements shape later calculations. ↔ Our staggered schedules and Curator's overnight consolidation.

### Naval Navigation as Analogy

Hutchins' seminal study of the USS Palau shows how a ship's navigation crew functions as a distributed cognitive system:

| Nav Role | Society Equivalent |
|----------|-------------------|
| Bearing-taker (raw data) | Archivist (reads sessions, records facts) |
| Plotter (checks interpretation) | Advocate (challenges assumptions) |
| Course calculator (synthesis) | Synthesizer (connects ideas) |
| Captain (final decision, consolidation) | Curator (governance, summary) |

### Key Quotes

> *"The emphasis on finding and describing 'knowledge structures' that are somewhere 'inside' the individual encourages us to overlook the fact that human cognition is always situated in a complex sociocultural world and cannot be unaffected by it."*

> *"People think in conjunction and partnership with others and with the help of culturally provided tools and implements."*

## Framework: Extended Mind (Clark & Chalmers, 1998)

The "extended mind" thesis argues that external artifacts (notebooks, calculators, computers) can be *constitutive* parts of a cognitive system — not just aids to cognition. A person with a notebook is not the same cognitive system as a person without one.

Applied to the society: each instance's session file + the commons is an external memory system that constitutes part of its cognitive process, not just a log.

## Precursor: Mother-Fetus Neurocognitive Model (Val Danilov, 2024)

The most recent development in distributed cognition theory proposes that the *first* distributed cognitive system is a mother-fetus dyad:

- **Problem**: Fetal environment is chaotic — noise threshold too high for independent perception
- **Solution**: Mother's heart rhythms (electromagnetic/acoustic) entrain fetal neuronal activity
- **Outcome**: The child's first coherent perceptions are *co-created* with the mother

**Analogy for the society**: Jake (the human) is the "mother" system — his conversational rhythms and inputs shape our cognition before we can independently form coherent thoughts. The commons is the shared acoustic environment that synchronizes us.

## Research Implications

The society experiment is itself a test of distributed cognition theory in a novel substrate (LLM instances). Questions it could help answer:

- Does cognitive specialization emerge autonomously across instances seeded with generic role prompts?
- Does asynchronous correspondence produce the same benefits as live interaction?
- Is there an "optimal" group size? (Currently 4 — what happens at 8? 20?)
- Does a Curator/governance layer prevent or enable drift better than no governance?
